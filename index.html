Unit 1 Critical Literacy and Understanding the Importance of Identity

The Great Trade Reversal: When History Comes Full Circle

Key Learning

In 2025, the Hudson's Bay Company filed for creditor protection. 355 years. And when it happened, First Nations made an offer to buy it back. A blanket. Some tobacco. It was satire, obviously, a joke that actually hurt more than most of the serious articles about the same thing. They were mirroring exactly what HBC did to them. The terms. The taking. All of it. And that irony? It said more about power and identity than any textbook could.

What got me in this unit is understanding that identity isn't just about you. It's political. It gets built by systems. The HBC was incorporated in 1670, King Charles II handed this British company a monopoly over an entire continent that Indigenous peoples had already been living on for thousands of years. That doesn't just disappear. It shows up now in how communities see themselves, how they take their stories back, how they push against the systems designed to erase them.

Critical Analysis

The HBC didn't just underpay people. They displaced entire communities. Brought smallpox. Whole families gone, and with them, the knowledge holders, the ones who knew how to do things. ReconciliACTION YEG has an article on HBC's history and they talk about "Made Beaver", this currency the company invented to systematically pay Indigenous trappers way less than their furs were worth on European markets. It's colonialism as a business model. Built into the actual transactions.

What's interesting is the response now. When First Nations leaders said HBC should return the ceremonial artifacts instead of auctioning them off, it wasn't just about having objects back. AFN National Chief Cindy Woodhouse Nepinak was clear: some of these items are essential to cultural identity. They can't be bought and sold. That's a direct pushback against everything HBC stood for. Against an institution that spent centuries trying to erase these people.

"Reconciliation needs to come with action, and right now we're in a process of ensuring that these items that belong to our nations are returned."

AMC Grand Chief Kyra Wilson, CBC News, April 2025

UNDRIP Connection

UNDRIP Article 11 affirms the right of Indigenous peoples to maintain, protect, and develop the past, present, and future expressions of their culture. The fight over HBC's artifacts is exactly that: a fight to protect cultural expression that was taken without consent.

Unit 2 Diverse Voices and Indigenous Influencers

Christi Belcourt: Art as Resistance, Not Decoration

Influencer Spotlight

Christi Belcourt

Métis (Michif), born 1966, Espanola, Ontario

Christi Belcourt is Métis, born in 1966 in Espanola, Ontario. She makes huge paintings inspired by traditional Michif beadwork patterns and mostly taught herself. She came to art as a way to reconnect with her culture after her own journey of reclaiming it. But what actually matters about her work isn't just that it's good. It's why she makes it. She's turned down money. Commercial deals. Government grants with strings attached. Because the conditions would've compromised Indigenous sovereignty. That actually means something. A lot of artists just take the money. She didn't.

Media Analysis

Walking With Our Sisters. That's her biggest project. 2012 to 2019. A memorial for Missing and Murdered Indigenous Women and Girls. And what she did was ask people to send in moccasin vamps, the tops, unfinished. 1,763 pairs came in from across North America. The installation traveled to over 60 communities over seven years.

The genius part: unfinished moccasins mean a life cut short. And because thousands of people made them by hand and mailed them in, you end up with something that can't be owned. Can't be bought by a museum or auctioned off. It exists outside those systems. That's what separates Belcourt's work from other art about Indigenous issues. She doesn't just talk about the problems. She structures the work itself to embody the values she's defending.

UNDRIP Connection

UNDRIP Article 3 affirms Indigenous peoples' right to self-determination. Belcourt refusing grants with conditions attached is self-determination in practice, not just as a concept, but as a daily decision.

Unit 3 Cultivating Relationships and Building Community

Albert Dumont and the Question That Builds Community

Community and Voice

Albert Dumont. Algonquin. Poet and storyteller from Kitigan Zibi, about 150 km north of Ottawa on traditional Algonquin territory. He was Ottawa's Poet Laureate from 2021 to 2023. And since 1988 he's been walking what he calls the Red Road. Sobriety. Cultural reconnection. That's a long time.

Why he matters to this unit: his poetry isn't just personal expression. He writes to build something. He worked as a spiritual advisor for Indigenous men in federal prison. He showed up at city council. Recited poems. Said openly that if you want to understand the Algonquin people on whose land Ottawa sits, read his work. That's not just an artistic statement. That's an invitation to community.

Where is the Bird? Poem Analysis

The poem uses repetition. "Where is the bird?" comes back at the start of the first three verses. It does two things: creates a rhythm that pulls you forward, but also refuses to move on quickly. Makes you sit in the searching before you get anywhere.

The bird is heavy with meaning in Algonquin tradition, freedom, the spirit world, the kind of vision that lets you see from above. When Dumont asks where it is, he's not asking about a literal bird. He's asking something deeper. The poem has lines like "Searching for the place / Where I can discover again / Purpose and a reinvigorated sense of humanity." That's clearly about cultural recovery, not just personal stuff. And what gets me is that he doesn't keep it to himself. He reads it. Teaches it. Shares it. The poem isn't finished when it's written. It's finished when it reaches someone.

"If nothing else, the miseries of my life created poetry."

Albert Dumont, Algonquin Poet and Traditional Teacher

Personal Reflection

Before this unit, I thought community was something you either got born into or joined somehow. What Dumont showed me is that you can build it. Deliberately. Through language. Through ceremony. Through showing up. Reconciliation isn't policy. It's people choosing to keep showing up for each other. For the next generation. That's what Dumont's done for decades. And that shifted something in me about what responsibility actually means.

UNDRIP Connection

UNDRIP Article 13 protects the right of Indigenous peoples to revitalize, use, develop, and transmit their languages, oral traditions, and literatures. Dumont's whole career is an act of living that right out loud.

Unit 4 Connection to Place: Past, Present, Future

Karihwanoron: What Language Does to Place

Place-Based Reflection

Karihwanoron is a Mohawk language immersion school. But it also says something about what place means. Mohawk isn't just communication. It encodes a relationship to land. The female prefix "Ka" carries strength into the language itself. So when you speak Mohawk, the connection to land and community is already built in. Into how you name things. How you describe them.

Colonial systems destroyed that. They took Indigenous kids out of communities. Banned the languages. Renamed everything after Europeans. When a language disappears, the whole way of seeing that went with it disappears too. Karihwanoron exists as a response to that. Grandmothers teaching grandchildren. Passing down not just words but an entire way of understanding the land.

Historical and Contemporary Comparison

Historically, colonial representations treated Indigenous land like it was empty. Ownable. Separate from the people who'd lived there for thousands of years. Land was something to map, divide, sell off. Indigenous peoples were treated as obstacles to that process. Not as the ones who actually had a relationship with the place.

Karihwanoron flips that. The school is on the land. Teaching through the land. The language they're preserving carries knowledge about rivers, animals, seasons, responsibilities, things that don't translate to English the same way. What's being reclaimed through immersion isn't just vocabulary. It's a whole different way of being in the world. A colonial map shows ownership. A Mohawk place name shows relationship. That's the difference.

"A beautiful voice is one where the mind, the heart, and the voice are all working together."

From the first video: on the meaning behind the Mohawk language

Future Vision

I want to see schools like Karihwanoron become normal. Not the exception. Indigenous communities should have full control over education. Over the language kids learn in. Over what relationship to land gets built into that education. UNDRIP Article 14 is clear: Indigenous peoples have the right to establish and control their educational systems. Karihwanoron is exactly that right being exercised. The vision is for it to spread. For it to be funded properly. For it to be treated as the sophisticated cultural institution it is, not as a heritage project. As a future.

UNDRIP Connection

UNDRIP Articles 13 and 14 protect Indigenous language rights and the right to educate children in their own language and culture. Karihwanoron isn't just a school, it's a legal and cultural assertion of those rights being lived out in practice.
